The University of Osaka

Master of Science in Mathematical Sciences

The program covered topics ranging from Bayesian statistics and multivariate analysis to stochastic processes and applied probability.

My master’s thesis focused on Piecewise Deterministic Markov Processes.

Abstract: Event-chain Monte Carlo (ECMC) methods are feasible for inference in massive datasets. These methods differ from traditional Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) in their sampling procedure and exact subsampling techniques. While in most MCMC methods the proposed samples are either accepted or rejected, ECMC simulates event times for which we can move our process along a deterministic trajectory. Considered to be a rejection-free non-reversible Markov chain Monte Carlo method, the implementation of ECMC is relatively simple when we can (1) efficiently compute the gradient of the negative logarithm of our target distribution and (2) easily sample event times. This work focuses on scenarios for which the gradient is complex to compute. We resort to gradient-free MCMC within the framework of ECMC to target complex variables. We achieve this by splitting the sampling procedure in a Gibbs-like manner. We apply this technique to a Gaussian process classification problem and compare the sampling performance of two possible combinations.

My bachelor's thesis focused on geothermics.

Abstract: The pressure necessary to (i) fracture a borehole wall and to (ii) connect drilling-induced fractures to each other can be simulated with information on the wellbore orientation and the stress field. In this work, acrylic blocks were fractured by nitrogen injection, simulating a wellbore undergoing drilling procedures. The experiments were performed under a combination of principal stresses and well deviation in order to better display fractures for direct observation. The first fracturing process resulted in a deviated wellbore whose axial fracture consisted of small parallel cracks linked according to the theoretical model. Despite this fracture connection, additional cracks appeared in the near-bore region. Further experiments were performed to explain the origin of these additional cracks. The results showed that unexpected fractures appeared due to material anisotropy, unloading process during experiments and the free-surface effect. We hope that the results here presented provide the basis for future experiments in rocks. Such experiments could then provide a fracture data analysis closer to the real-world setting.
